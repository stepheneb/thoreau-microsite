Thoreau spent a night in prison
after refusing to pay a tax
in July of 1846,
unwilling to support a government
that supported slavery.
This struggle against injustice led
Thoreau to write one of the most widely
read and influential essays about
civil disobedience.

One of his friends Bronson Alcott had come to town a few years before and spoken about political action, for instance, how could one pay one’s taxes to a government which justified the holding of slaves? So Thoreau had stopped paying his taxes…

Laura Dassow Walls, Historian

He walked into town, and famously Sam Staples accosted him and said, “you need to pay your taxes and if you won’t, I need to put you in jail.” When Thoreau was released from jail the next morning, walking through the town, talking to people, he realized that they didn’t understand why he’d taken the stance that he had.

Laura Dassow Walls, Historian

He wrote a long lecture, what became Civil Disobedience, and he laid it all out for them. And the principle is very simple. It’s that you do not lend yourself to a injustice that you condemn.

Who was Thoreau?

Henry David Thoreau (1817-1862) observed and wrote expansively about the natural world. In 1845, he went to lived in a house by Walden Pond “to front only the essential facts of life.” His most productive period as a writer began during the two years he spent living deliberately in the woods.

Thoreau’s name has become synonymous with two themes: love of nature and uncompromising ethical values. Thoreau believed that the attempt to understand human concerns in the context of nature helped provide guidance for the proper conduct of life. He directed his career as a writer toward making that realization possible for others.
